# HR-Analytics

## PROJECT OVERVIEW
- Case Study: Employee Attrition Analysis at TechSolutions Pvt. Ltd.
- Data Source: HR Analytics datasets (Excel)
- Tool Used: Excel, Power BI, Tableau, MySQL
- Goals: Understand attrition trends & provide actionable insights.


## PROBLEM STATEMENT
- The organization is facing a high employee attrition rate of ~50% across departments, with over 25,000 employees leaving out of 50,000. 
- Despite long average tenure (~20 years) and relatively consistent work-life balance scores (~2.5), attrition is consistently high across Software, Sales, Support, HR, R&D, and Hardware.

## DATASET USED
<a href="">HR-Analytics-Excel</a>

## OBJECTIVES OF THE PROJECT
- Identify department-wise attrition.
- Explore links between promotions, income & attrition.
- Analyze compensation & satisfaction trends
- Visualize data using different BI tools to extract actionable insights.
- Present findings in an interactive, business-friendly format.

## CHALLENGES FACED DURING PROJECT
- EXCEL:
    - Handling large datasets slowed down pivot tables and charts.
    - Choosing the right chart type (e.g., line vs histogram for promotions).
    - Balancing clarity vs aesthetics on dashboards (too much data caused clutter
- POWER BI:
    - Creating custom DAX measures for attrition and promotions was challenging.
    - Performance issues with larger dataset while applying filters.
    - Designing a dashboard that was both interactive and easy-to-read.
- TABLEAU:
    - Optimizing extracts for performance (slow load with larger datasets).
    - Creating meaningful story dashboards without overwhelming the audience.
    - Limited customization compared to Power BI for certain visuals.
- SQL:
    - Complex joins needed between multiple tables (HR_1 & HR_2).
    - Writing queries for HR metrics (attrition %, tenure, promotions) required accuracy in grouping & filtering.

  ## KEY INSIGHTS & FINDINGS
  - High Attrition Departments:
    - R&D, Software, and Support have attrition rates above 50%.
  - Salary Not the Main Driver:
    - Employees who left vs. stayed had similar average income (~$26K).
  - Work-Life Balance Stable:
    - Average score ~2.5/4 across roles — not a major factor for leaving.
  - Promotion Gaps Strongly Linked to Attrition:
    - Employees with longer gaps since last promotion were much more likely to leave.
  - Mid-Career Employees at Higher Risk:
    - Age group 26–35 and early tenure employees had the highest turnover.
  - Job Satisfaction Matters:
    - Tableau insights showed lower satisfaction scores in high-attrition groups.
